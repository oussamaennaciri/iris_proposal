\begin{abstract}
The increasing frequency of geopolitical instability, interstate conflict, and
institutional breakdown has intensified the demand for timely, evidence-based
intelligence tools accessible to multilateral organizations and international
agencies. Existing systems such as Palantir Gotham and Bloomberg Terminal are
proprietary, nationally oriented, or financially motivated, leaving the United
Nations, humanitarian agencies, and neutral multilateral bodies without open,
transparent alternatives. This project proposes the design and development of
the International Risk Intelligence System, a near-real-time geopolitical
intelligence platform that ingests, integrates, and models data from nine open
international data sources to produce country-level risk scores, anomaly
alerts, and relationship graphs for global decision support.

The system is built across four architectural layers: a multi-source ingestion
pipeline orchestrated with Apache Airflow, a knowledge graph engine built on
NetworkX and Neo4j, a machine learning layer consisting of an XGBoost risk
scorer and an Isolation Forest anomaly detector, and a Streamlit dashboard
delivering live visualizations, a world risk heatmap, a country event feed,
and an alert panel. Data sources include the GDELT Project, ACLED, the World
Bank API, UN Comtrade, the UNHCR Refugee Statistics API, OpenSanctions, the
UN General Assembly Voting dataset, the Varieties of Democracy dataset, and
the Geopolitical Risk Index. The system is benchmarked against established
geopolitical risk indices and validated on known historical conflict onset
events. The anticipated contribution is a transparent, open-source, globally
neutral intelligence tool that addresses a structural gap in the multilateral
decision-making ecosystem.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Background}

The post-Cold War multilateral order has faced sustained pressure from rising
nationalist movements, great power competition, and the erosion of
international legal frameworks. The United Nations Secretary-General's 2023
report on the reform of the multilateral system identified a critical
operational gap: the absence of accessible, neutral, and data-driven tools to
support early warning and collective action among member states. While
intelligence-grade platforms such as Palantir Gotham serve national governments
with classified infrastructure, and financial terminals such as Bloomberg
aggregate geopolitical signals for market participants, no equivalent
open-access system exists for multilateral institutions whose mandates require
political neutrality and global coverage.

Academic research has demonstrated the feasibility of machine learning
approaches to geopolitical risk prediction. Projects such as the VIEWS
forecasting system \cite{views2023}, the EU Global Conflict Risk Index
\cite{gcri2020}, and ACLED's Conflict Alert System have individually shown that
open data sources can generate reliable conflict forecasts up to 36 months in
advance. However, these systems remain siloed, domain-specific, and
inaccessible as unified platforms for operational decision support. None
integrates the full spectrum of geopolitical signals including trade
dependency, political alignment, sanction networks, displacement flows, and
regime stability into a single real-time intelligence layer.

This project addresses that gap by building a full-stack geopolitical
intelligence system grounded in open data, reproducible methods, and
transparent modeling. The system is designed with the United Nations, the
International Committee of the Red Cross, the World Bank, Amnesty
International, and foreign ministries of non-aligned states as its primary
users. Its architecture is intentionally open and cloud-deployable, making it
structurally accessible to any organization regardless of national affiliation
or budget.

\section{Problem Statement}

The central question this project addresses is: \textit{how can heterogeneous,
multi-source open data be integrated in near-real-time to generate accurate,
explainable, and actionable country-level geopolitical risk intelligence for
multilateral decision-making?}

This problem is significant for three reasons. First, geopolitical instability
is accelerating. ACLED documented over 170,000 conflict events globally in
2024 alone, and the UNHCR reported a record 117 million forcibly displaced
persons as of early 2024. Decision makers at international agencies require
signals before crises peak, not after. Second, the data required to generate
such signals is already publicly available across multiple sources but has
never been unified into a single operational system oriented toward multilateral
use. Third, existing risk indices are either static, nationally biased, or
proprietary, making them unsuitable for the continuous and neutral intelligence
function that multilateral agencies require.

The system addresses all phases of the data lifecycle. Collection involves
pulling from nine open APIs and datasets covering events, economics, trade,
displacement, sanctions, voting, and regime characteristics. Preparation
involves schema normalization, temporal alignment, and feature engineering
across heterogeneous source formats. Exploration involves country-level
exploratory data analysis and correlation mapping across sources. Modeling
involves training and validating two machine learning models: a risk scorer
producing continuous instability scores and an anomaly detector flagging
unexpected trajectory shifts. Visualization involves a live dashboard
delivering a world heatmap, country drill-downs, a knowledge graph, and an
alert panel. Reporting involves structured country briefings generated from
model outputs.

The successful delivery of this system would provide the first open, neutral,
multi-source geopolitical risk intelligence platform purpose-built for
multilateral institutions, and one that is directly comparable in scope to
commercially deployed systems such as Recorded Future and Verisk Maplecroft
while remaining fully accessible to non-governmental and intergovernmental
organizations.

\section{Related Work}

Significant academic and institutional work has established the theoretical and
empirical foundations on which this project builds. This section reviews five
representative contributions that directly inform the system design, and
identifies the limitations that the International Risk Intelligence System is
positioned to address.

The VIEWS (Violence and Impacts Early Warning System) project \cite{views2023}
represents the most comprehensive existing open-source conflict prediction
effort, generating monthly probabilistic forecasts of political violence one to
36 months ahead at national and subnational levels for all countries globally.
VIEWS demonstrates that open data can support institutional early warning at
scale and is actively used by UN agencies. However, VIEWS is a forecasting
model, not an operational intelligence platform. It produces statistical
outputs without a unified data pipeline, graph layer, or decision-support
interface, and does not integrate economic, trade, or diplomatic alignment
signals into its risk estimates.

Iacoviello and colleagues \cite{iacoviello2025} introduced a high-frequency
news-based indicator framework covering geopolitical risk, economic policy
uncertainty, and political sentiment across 42 countries, demonstrating that
Random Forest models incorporating such indicators significantly outperform
traditional econometric baselines in predicting sovereign risk. Their work
establishes the methodological validity of the machine learning approach
adopted in this project and motivates the inclusion of news sentiment and
economic indicators as core features of the risk scorer. Related work by
Filetti and colleagues \cite{tft2025} further demonstrates that hybrid
architectures combining temporal fusion transformers with probabilistic models
outperform single-model baselines on GDELT-based conflict forecasting tasks.

The Global Conflict Risk Index \cite{gcri2020}, developed by the European
Commission Joint Research Centre, serves as the quantitative foundation of the
EU Conflict Early Warning System and evaluates conflict risk across political,
economic, social, environmental, and security dimensions for all UN member
states. The GCRI demonstrates institutional appetite for quantitative risk
tools and provides a validated feature framework that informs this project's
feature engineering design. However, the GCRI is updated infrequently and is
not designed for real-time operational use. The promise of machine learning for
conflict forecasting more broadly is reviewed by Randahl and Vegelius
\cite{mlconflict2024}, who document consistent accuracy improvements over
expert judgment baselines while identifying key gaps in explainability and
operational accessibility.

Rudolph and colleagues \cite{rudolph2026} proposed a temporal knowledge graph
architecture for geopolitical event forecasting using GDELT, ACLED, ICEWS, and
POLECAT, demonstrating that representing geopolitical events as structured
graph quadruples of entity, relation, and timestamp enables more accurate
temporal reasoning than flat event tables. This work directly motivates the
graph layer of the International Risk Intelligence System and validates the use
of Neo4j and NetworkX for persistent relationship storage and analysis.
Complementary temporal graph analysis by Meier and colleagues
\cite{temporalgraph2025} on 25 years of geopolitical export control dynamics
further demonstrates the value of graph-theoretic approaches for detecting
patterns of escalation and coordination across political actors.

The SAGE hybrid forecasting system \cite{sage2024} demonstrated that combining
machine-generated probabilistic forecasts with human analyst assessments
produces superior accuracy relative to either approach alone. While this
project does not implement a human-in-the-loop component in its initial
version, the SAGE findings motivate the explainability design of the risk
scorer, which outputs feature importance rankings alongside scores so that
analysts can interrogate and contextualize model outputs. Additional supporting
evidence for the multi-source pipeline design is provided by Scotti and
colleagues \cite{migration2025}, who demonstrate that ACLED-based conflict
forecasts integrated with socioeconomic variables significantly improve
forced migration predictions, validating the cross-domain data fusion approach
adopted here. The early warning displacement model of Schutte and colleagues
\cite{displacement2025} and the GDELT norm mining methodology of Norbutas and
Corten \cite{gdelt2020} further support the use of multi-source event data
for extracting structured political signals at scale.

Collectively, these works establish that open data can support rigorous
geopolitical risk modeling, that machine learning outperforms traditional
methods on such tasks, and that graph-based representations of political
relationships improve forecasting accuracy. The International Risk Intelligence
System synthesizes these findings into a unified, operational, multilateral-
facing platform that none of the reviewed works individually provides.

\section{Methodology and Approach}
\textcolor{red}{Note: This section will be completed at the end of the course
with full documentation of implemented methods, training procedures,
hyperparameter tuning, and evaluation results.}

The system will be implemented in Python across four layers: a multi-source
ingestion pipeline, a graph engine, a machine learning layer, and an
interactive dashboard. The ingestion pipeline will use Apache Airflow for
scheduling and orchestration. The graph engine will use NetworkX for in-memory
analysis and Neo4j for persistent storage. The machine learning layer will
train an XGBoost risk scorer and an Isolation Forest anomaly detector. The
dashboard will be built with Streamlit and Pyvis. All code will be
version-controlled on GitHub with full documentation.

\section{Expected Outcomes}

This project is expected to deliver five concrete outputs by the end of Week 8.

The first output is a fully operational nine-source data ingestion pipeline
orchestrated with Apache Airflow, capable of pulling, normalizing, and storing
heterogeneous data across update schedules ranging from 15-minute intervals to
annual releases. This pipeline will be reproducible, documented, and
version-controlled on GitHub with a complete README and setup instructions.

The second output is a country-level geopolitical risk scorer built on
XGBoost, trained against historical GPR Index values and validated on holdout
conflict onset events including documented cases of sudden conflict escalation.
The model will produce a continuous instability score from 0 to 100 for every
UN member state on each pipeline run, accompanied by feature importance
rankings that allow analysts to understand the drivers of each score.

The third output is an anomaly detector built on Isolation Forest, monitoring
the risk score time-series of each country and generating alerts when
trajectories deviate significantly from historical baselines. The detector
will be validated against documented historical shock events and will output a
binary alert, a severity level, and the primary feature driving the deviation.

The fourth output is a geopolitical knowledge graph stored in Neo4j and
analyzed with NetworkX, representing countries, leaders, and organizations as
nodes and treaties, trade relationships, sanctions, voting alignments, and
conflict events as edges. The graph will be updated on each pipeline run and
queryable for bilateral and multilateral relationship analysis.

The fifth output is a Streamlit web dashboard integrating all system outputs
into a single operational interface, including a world risk heatmap updated in
near-real-time, country-level drill-down panels, a live event feed, an alert
panel surfacing anomaly detector outputs, and a Pyvis-powered interactive
graph visualization.

A realistic limitation of this project is that the risk scorer's ground truth
labels are drawn from existing indices that themselves carry methodological
assumptions and coverage gaps. The system's outputs should be interpreted as
decision support signals rather than definitive assessments, and future work
should incorporate human analyst feedback loops as demonstrated by the SAGE
system \cite{sage2024}.
